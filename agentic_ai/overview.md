# Agentic AI in Economics Research

This chapter focuses on the use of **agentic AI** — AI systems that can plan, use tools, and execute multi-step tasks autonomously — as tools for enhancing economics research productivity.

Agentic AI represents a significant step beyond conversational chatbots. Rather than simply responding to prompts, these systems can decompose complex research questions, search the web, fetch and analyze data, write and debug code, and synthesize findings — all with limited human intervention. For economists, this opens possibilities ranging from automated literature reviews to end-to-end empirical analysis.

## What is an AI Agent?

An AI agent is a software system that combines a large language model (LLM) with:

- **Planning capabilities** — the ability to decompose a goal into a sequence of steps
- **Tool access** — the ability to call external functions (web search, code execution, database queries, file system access)
- **Memory** — storage that persists across steps in a workflow

The key distinction from a chatbot is autonomy. A chatbot waits for your next message. An agent, once given a goal, can pursue it through multiple steps without requiring input at each turn.

## Why This Matters for Economists

Agentic AI is relevant to economists at every career stage:

- **Literature reviews** that once took weeks can be completed in under an hour using Deep Research agents
- **Data retrieval and analysis** can be automated end-to-end — from identifying the right data source to producing figures and tables
- **Econometric code** can be written, debugged, and refined through natural language instructions ("vibe coding")
- **Research workflows** spanning multiple tasks can be orchestrated by a lead agent that coordinates specialized subagents

At the same time, current agents have real limitations: they hallucinate, they can propagate errors across a multi-step workflow, and they sometimes reproduce common misconceptions from their training data rather than applying rigorous economic reasoning. Korinek's useful analogy: treat them like a team of research assistants — talented, fast, but requiring careful oversight and verification of outputs.
